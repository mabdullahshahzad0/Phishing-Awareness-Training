The Basics

What Is Phishing?

Before spotting an attack, it helps to understand exactly what you're up against.

  • 01
    A cyberattack that tricks people into revealing sensitive information, credentials, or installing malware.
  • 02
    Attackers impersonate trusted brands, coworkers, banks, or institutions to earn quick trust.
  • 03
    The leading entry point for ransomware, account takeovers, and large-scale data breaches.
  • 04
    It targets people, not just systems — success depends on psychology as much as technology.
#1
Cause of data breaches worldwide

Billions of phishing emails are sent every single day — most filtered silently, but it only takes one click to cause real damage.

Know Your Enemy

Types of Phishing Attacks

Different disguises, same goal: steal information or access.

Email Phishing

Mass fraudulent emails impersonating trusted senders.

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks built on personal details.

Whaling

Attacks aimed squarely at executives and leadership.

Smishing

Phishing delivered through SMS text messages.

Vishing

Voice calls impersonating banks or support desks.

Clone Phishing

A real email is copied and its links swapped.

Spot It

How to Spot a Fake Website

Before you type in a password, take five seconds to check.

Check the URL carefully

Watch for misspellings or extra characters, like paypa1.com instead of paypal.com.

Look for HTTPS & the padlock

A good sign — but not proof. Attackers can fake these too.

Inspect the design quality

Broken logos, odd layouts, or inconsistent branding are warning signs.

No verifiable contact info

Missing About, Contact, or Privacy pages are a red flag.

Unexpected pop-ups

Fake urgent warnings or "you've won" messages signal a scam.

Type the address yourself

Don't rely on links in emails or texts — navigate manually.

The Psychology

Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing isn't a technology problem first — it's a psychology problem.

Authority
Impersonating a boss, bank, or government official to demand compliance.
Urgency
"Act now or your account will be suspended" — no time to think.
Fear
Threatening legal action, fines, or loss of access to pressure a reaction.
Curiosity
Enticing subject lines like "You won a prize!" to bait a click.
Trust & Familiarity
Mimicking a coworker, friend, or well-known brand to lower your guard.
Why It Matters

Real-World Attack Patterns

These aren't hypothetical — variations of each happen every day.

Business Email Compromise

Attackers impersonate executives or vendors to trick finance teams into wiring company funds to fraudulent accounts — one of the costliest phishing patterns for organizations worldwide.

Credential Spear-Phishing

Employees receive convincing, personalized messages designed to harvest login credentials, giving attackers a foothold into internal systems and tools.

Smishing Delivery Scams

Fake "missed delivery" texts link to cloned courier websites that harvest payment details from unsuspecting recipients.

Defend Yourself

Best Practices & Tips

Small habits that block almost every phishing attempt.

Pause before you click — verify the sender and the link first

Hover to preview a link's real destination before trusting it

Never share passwords or OTPs over email, text, or phone

Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on every account

Keep software, browsers, and antivirus fully updated

Use a password manager to keep every password unique

Report suspicious messages to IT/security — don't just delete

When in doubt, verify through a separate, trusted channel
